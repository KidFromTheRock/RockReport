Recruiting Has a Calendar. Families Need to Know It.

College recruiting can feel random from the outside. One week a coach is texting. Another week no one seems to be moving. The truth is more structured: NCAA rules shape when and how coaches can communicate, evaluate, visit, invite and recruit.

That is why the recruiting calendar matters. It is not just a compliance document for coaches. It is a planning tool for families.

The NCAA explains that recruiting looks different by division and sport, and that families should understand several basics before the process gets serious: coaches initiate most recruiting conversations, exposure matters, academics are part of the decision, and communication rules vary by division and grade level.

What families should do with the calendar

Do not treat the calendar as something to check only when a coach mentions it. Use it to work backward.

  • Before contact windows open: clean up film, update grades, confirm measurables and make sure the recruiting profile is accurate.
  • Before evaluation periods: know which camps, combines, 7-on-7 events or showcases are actually worth the time and money.
  • Before visits: prepare questions about fit, academics, roster needs, position development and the real timeline.
  • Before offers or aid conversations: understand what is written, what is verbal and what should be reviewed by someone qualified.

The short view: Recruiting is easier to manage when families stop reacting to every text or camp invite and start planning around the actual calendar.

The calendar does not guarantee opportunity. But it does help families avoid one of the biggest mistakes in recruiting: waiting until the process is already moving to get organized.

Start with the official NCAA recruiting page, then build a family calendar around the sport, division level and class year that applies to the athlete.
