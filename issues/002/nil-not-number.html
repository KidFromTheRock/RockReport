Money Game

A Welcome From The Seiler Group

Money Game presented by The Seiler Group of Raymond James

For families, an NIL or revenue-sharing opportunity can feel like a celebration and a new responsibility at the same time.

Seeing your son receive college football scholarship offers is an exciting milestone. Seeing him become a student-athlete who secures his first Name, Image and Likeness opportunity — or begins to navigate revenue-sharing conversations — adds another layer. It can be a reward for years of hard work, early mornings and family dedication.

But this moment also introduces real financial questions. Unlike a traditional campus job, NIL and revenue-sharing money may not come with the same simple paycheck structure families are used to seeing. It requires organization, planning and a better understanding of the responsibilities that can come with new income.

A note from The Seiler Group of Raymond James

Welcome to The Seiler Group of Raymond James. We are excited to contribute to The Rock Report and help families think through the financial questions that can come with this new era of college football.

Our goal in this space is education. Each week, we’ll look at practical topics families may need to understand as student-athletes move through recruiting, NIL, revenue sharing and the transition into college life.

The bottom line

NIL and revenue-sharing opportunities can be powerful tools to help a student-athlete begin building financial independence. But the opportunity is stronger when families treat it with the seriousness of a business decision.

That means asking better questions, keeping clear records, understanding potential tax responsibilities and making sure the athlete is protected before anything is signed.

The short view: A first NIL deal should not just feel exciting. It should also start a family conversation about planning, protection and long-term decision-making.

In the coming weeks and months, The Seiler Group will address questions that help families better understand the choices they may face around NIL, revenue sharing and financial responsibility. Some topics may stand alone. Others may become a series when they need more explanation.

Note: This article is educational only and should not be read as financial, legal or tax advice. Families should consult qualified professionals for advice specific to their situation.
